Abstract. We present a finite element method for a generalized version of the Navier-Stokes6
equations that is applicable to (highly permeable) porous media flows. We rely on the variational7
multiscale (VMS) framework to produce a stabilized numerical method that allows the use of equal-8
order finite element spaces for all the problem unknowns, while also preventing the instabilities9
associated to convection-dominated flows or strong reaction terms. Two variants of the basic algo-10
rithm are considered and tested in a selection of numerical experiments designed to examine their11
performance when changing the relative magnitudes of the different terms in the momentum balance12
equation. 13

1. Introduction. Porous media flows are typically modelled by a pointwise gen-17
eralization of the classical Darcy equation, which results from postulating that the18
flow is in a state of permanent local mechanical equilibrium, with the pressure gradi-19
ent and external body forces balancing the interfacial viscous resistance caused by the20
fluidвҖҷs motion relative to the porous matrix. In these conditions, the fluidвҖҷs inertia,21
as well as the contribution of the viscous forces arising from the fluid motion relative22
to itself, can be neglected.23
The adequacy of the Darcy model in describing a wide range of porous media24
flows is empirically well established and has even been rigorously derived in a number25
of idealized scenarios by applying homogenization theory, with the Stokes equations26
as a description of the microscopic flow [20, 6]. However, the underlying assumptions27
of negligible inertia and clear separation of scales fail to hold in a number of practical28
scenarios encountered in the oil and gas [33, 30], biomedical [28, 18] or food [31] indus-29
tries, to name but a few. Nonetheless, the application of homogenization theory under30
relaxed assumptions is still possible, yielding generalized equations that encompass31
the Darcy equation as a limiting case [7, 5].32
Non-Darcy effects can be mathematically captured by incorporating a more so-33
phisticated, nonlinear resistance term into the momentum conservation equation,34
along with additional terms: the inertia term that stems from taking the material35
derivative of the flow when describing the local conservation of momentum in an Eu-36
lerian framework, and a viscous term (the Brinkman term) representing the intra-fluid37
viscous forces 1. As a result, the equations of motion acquire the basic form of the38
Navier-Stokes equations for incompressible flow (generalized to include a viscous re-39
sistance term), with modifications to account for the varying porosity that affect both40
the momentum and mass conservation equations [3]. A widely used model conform-41
ing to the preceding description is that defined by the Darcy-Brinkman-Forchheimer42

From the numerical standpoint, the generalized equations bring about all the well-46
known problems associated with the discretization of the generalized Navier-Stokes47
system. That is, the Galerkin form of their finite element discretization suffers from48
instabilities that appear when the viscous term is dominated by any of the lower-order49
terms such as in convection-dominated or reaction-dominated (i.e, dominated by the50
term that is proportional to the fluid velocity by analogy with classical convection-51
diffusion-reaction systems) flows. Furthermore, the use of equal-order interpolations52
for the velocity and the pressure leads to the violation of the Ladyzhenskaya-BabuЛҮ ska-53
Brezzi (LBB) condition, requiring stabilization regardless of the situation.54
For the Navier-Stokes system, all of these numerical issues have been successfully55
addressed in the past [10, 11] using the Variational Multi-scale (VMS) framework [22]56
to design stabilized methods. In [10], the so-called Algebraic Sub-Grid Scale (ASGS)57
formulation is applied to the Navier-Stokes system, including a reactive term (as well58
as additional terms related to Coriolis forces which we will not consider here), where59
optimal error estimates are proven for the linearized problem.60
Our goal in this work is to present a numerical method for single-phase, highly61
permeable porous media flows. We will concentrate in the time-independent problem62
here, leaving time dependence considerations (e.g., of the porosity field) to future63
work. To accomplish this, we generalize the formulation presented in [10], allowing for64
the presence of an externally-imposed porosity field, and analyze the extent to which65
the various results obtained in that work carry over to the present setting. Indeed,66
our analysis shows that essentially the same stability and convergence properties are67
preserved for the problem analyzed herein if the porosity field is smooth and has68
bounded gradients that are sufficiently resolved by the mesh. This conclusion is69
backed up by a battery of numerical tests that explore the robustness of the method70
with respect to changes in the physical parameters. Over the course of drafting the71
present paper, we came across the work [23], which applies VMS to the so-called72
Navier-Stokes-Brinkman system, applicable to highly-permeable porous media flows73
with a uniform porosity field. Except for the presence of a nonlinear reaction term,74
the theory in [10] fully applies to this case, so no need for revisiting the theory was75
required there.76
The formulation presented here is general enough to encompass alternative vari-77
ants of the VMS formulation, defined by different choices of the space where the78
sub-grid scales (SGS) live (see subsection 3.2). This generality can be useful for re-79
searchers or engineers looking to explore alternative possibilities to the basic method.80
In particular, we consider the so-called Orthogonal Sub-Grid Scale (OSGS) [11, 12]81
alongside the ASGS in all our numerical tests.82
Our approach has some advantages over previous efforts that were able to succes-83
sively address the numerical challenges mentioned above, including the use of inf-sup84
stable element pairs [8], with the associated complexity increase in the associated85
data structures required; or the only precedent of a stabilized finite element method:86
the Local Projection Stabilization [29], which, apart from requiring the use of special87
enrichment functions, introduces some constraints on the mesh topology.88

8. Conclusions. We have applied the VMS framework to generalize the formu-924
lation and analysis presented in [10] to the porous Navier-Stokes system, in such a925
way that the original method is recovered when Оұ вҖқ 1. By using the abstract no-926
tation introduced in later works on VMS [15], we have strived to make it clearer to927
the less versed reader how the whole process is largely systematic once the particular928
equations are fit to the general framework.929
Our analysis and numerical experiments show that the convergence properties930
of the original Navier-Stokes formulation are essentially preserved in the generalized931
setting. The analytical results have been corroborated in the numerical experiments,932
which show that the method remains just as robust in front of extreme variations933
in the physical parameters in the general case, as it is well-established to be for the934
original problem.935
The specialization of the robustness analysis to equal-order polynomial elements936
presented in section 6 shows a few details previously not discussed, such as the mech-937
anism of pressure error improvement with growing Da h or the (very weak in prac-938
tice) deterioration of the velocity error with Da. By normalizing all the variables939
adequately, we have shown that the absolute errors are very stable with respect to940
changes in the physical parameters when the convergence rate is similar.941
The abstract framework favored here makes it natural to include different partic-942
ularizations of VMS in a very concise way. This has allowed us to implement both the943
ASGS and the OSGS versions of the method, showing through the many numerical944
experiments that both variants have very similar properties, at least for the problem945
considered.946
There are several directions in which we think it is interesting to take the for-947
mulation developed here. First, we would like to study the possibility of simplifying948
the stabilized linear forms, given that not all the terms included are strictly neces-949
sary to obtain optimal convergence rates. In this sense, the so-called term-by-term950
stabilization approach [12] is a promising alternative.951
Moreover, we are interested in studying the possibility of rewriting the formulation952
to make it suitable for large porosity gradients, including the possibility of consid-953
ering discontinuous step-like changes, which have many engineering applications. As954
pointed out in [8], such formulations would likely require the integration by parts of955
the terms involving вҲҮОұ in order to weaken the smoothness requirements of this field.956
Another question not considered above is the effect of using the finite elements957
to interpolate the porosity field, as it would be natural to do in some applications.958
